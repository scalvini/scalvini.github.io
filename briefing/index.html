Part I — Week in Review (4–9 May)

1. The Big Picture

A volatile week dominated by the US–Iran conflict. Monday opened with a sell-off after the UAE intercepted Iranian missiles, pushing Brent above $116 and the Dow down 557 points. By mid-week a fragile ceasefire framework emerged, crushing crude back toward $101 and igniting a furious equity rally. Strong tech earnings — AMD crossed a $713 billion market cap — propelled the Nasdaq to six consecutive weekly gains (+4.5% on the week), while the S&P 500 closed at a fresh all-time high of 7,399. In Europe, the FTSE MIB outperformed (+2.2%) on energy-cost relief, though the FTSE 100 ended lower (−1.3%) as Shell and BP dragged. The macro backdrop is increasingly conflicted: labour markets remain robust (strong US April payrolls), but inflation is re-accelerating on energy, leaving central banks frozen.

7. Key Stories of the Week

US–Iran ceasefire framework. The week's dominant macro driver. Monday's UAE missile intercept sent Brent above $116 and equities lower. By Wednesday, credible signals of a framework agreement pulled crude back toward $101, but renewed clashes on Thursday cast doubt on durability. Oil ended the week around $101 — down sharply but still elevated versus pre-conflict levels.

Amazon Supply Chain Services launch. Amazon unveiled a full-stack logistics business (freight, distribution, fulfilment, parcel shipping). GXO Logistics fell 11%, UPS −10%, FedEx and C.H. Robinson each −9%. A structural threat to the logistics sector.

AMD breaks $700 billion. The chipmaker's market cap reached $713 billion after a 20% weekly surge on strong earnings, underscoring continued AI/semiconductor demand.

US April payrolls beat. Friday's jobs report topped expectations, reinforcing the "strong labour market + sticky inflation" narrative that keeps the Fed in a holding pattern.

Trump–EU tariff threats. The president warned of "much higher" tariffs if the EU doesn't eliminate levies on US goods — a headwind for European exporters and the DAX, which managed only +0.2% on the week.

9. Catalysts & Risks

US CPI on Tuesday is the week's marquee release. Consensus expects 3.7% y/y, which would be the highest print since 2023 and confirm energy's feed-through into broader inflation. A hot number could pull rate-cut expectations further out and pressure equities; a cooler print would be a relief rally catalyst. Core CPI at +0.4% m/m would signal breadth beyond energy.

Fed leadership transition. Kevin Warsh's Senate confirmation vote is expected this week. Powell's chairmanship formally ends 15 May, though he'll remain as governor. Any policy-signalling from Warsh during confirmation could move rates.

Iran ceasefire durability. The fragile framework remains the wild card. Renewed hostilities could send Brent back above $110 quickly, reversing the week's equity gains and re-widening the BTP spread as Italian energy costs spike.

10. Portfolio Positioning Notes

Fixed income (50%): The BTP spread at ~80 bp is manageable but warrants attention. If the ECB signals a June hike more strongly, short-duration BTPs may underperform. Consider whether maturing bonds should be rolled into shorter tenors or inflation-linked BTPi given the 3% CPI backdrop. Rising Bund yields (3.00%) offer a German alternative that was unthinkable two years ago.

Equity (50% — VOO + TDIV): Both ETFs are near all-time highs. The six-week S&P winning streak and subdued VIX (17) suggest complacency. Tuesday's CPI is the near-term binary event — a beat could trigger a 1–2% pullback. TDIV's tech-heavy, dividend-paying profile has been a sweet spot in 2026 (+22% YTD), but concentration in semiconductors introduces sector-specific risk if the AI capex cycle slows.

11. Eurozone Macro Snapshot

Eurozone inflation at 3% is now meaningfully above the ECB's 2% target, driven primarily by energy. The ECB's April press conference revealed an unusual level of internal debate about hiking — a sharp pivot from the easing bias of late 2025. Growth risks are tilted to the downside as the Iran conflict disrupts energy supply chains and Trump's tariff threats cloud the export outlook. Italy is the most vulnerable member state given its gas dependency and fiscal constraints ahead of 2027 elections. The labour market remains resilient across the bloc, complicating the ECB's calculus.

12. Bottom Line

Markets shrugged off a geopolitical scare and rallied hard on tech earnings and ceasefire hopes, but the inflation picture is deteriorating. Tuesday's US CPI print is the week's defining event — a hot number would validate the ECB's hawkish pivot and challenge the equity rally's foundation. The portfolio's barbell structure (Italian govt bonds + US tech-heavy equity) is well-positioned if the ceasefire holds and inflation stabilises, but vulnerable to a scenario where energy costs re-surge, widening the BTP spread while compressing equity multiples. Key watchpoint: if Brent reclaims $110, consider trimming equity exposure and extending BTP duration locks.
