Week in Review
  • US equities hit fresh all-time highs, with the S&P 500 crossing 7,200 for the first time, capping the strongest monthly performance since November 2020.
  • European markets fell sharply (DAX, CAC 40, FTSE MIB all down over 2%) as the Iran war energy shock weighed on the continent's growth outlook.
  • Brent crude surged past $108 after the World Bank warned the conflict would trigger the largest energy price surge since 2022.
  • Both the ECB and the BoE held rates steady, but eurozone inflation jumped to 3.0% in April, fuelling expectations of an ECB hike in June.

A striking transatlantic divergence defined the week. In the US, a powerful earnings season and renewed enthusiasm for AI spending propelled the S&P 500 and Nasdaq to record closes. Apple's fiscal Q2 beat (revenue of $111.2bn, guidance well above consensus) was the week's catalyst, lifting the entire tech complex on Friday. Around 84% of S&P 500 companies that reported beat earnings expectations, according to FactSet.

Europe told a different story. German business sentiment dropped to its lowest since the pandemic (Ifo at 84.4), while eurozone Q1 GDP growth slowed to 0.8% year on year. The continued fallout from the Strait of Hormuz closure, which has disrupted roughly 20% of global oil supply, is feeding directly into European energy costs and consumer prices. Flash eurozone CPI for April came in at 3.0%, up sharply from recent months, though core inflation held at 2.2%.

Oil remained the week's dominant macro variable. WTI rose over 7% on the week as ceasefire talks stalled. Gold benefited from the risk backdrop, climbing to $4,645. The VIX, notably, drifted lower to 17.0, suggesting US equity markets continue to price in a contained conflict.

Analytical Theme: The ECB's June Dilemma

The ECB faces its most consequential decision in two years at the 5 June meeting. Eurozone inflation at 3.0% is unambiguously above target, and the energy shock shows no sign of abating with the Strait of Hormuz still closed. Yet growth is already slowing (0.8% y/y in Q1), and the Ifo index's plunge to pandemic-era lows suggests worse ahead. The textbook says you do not hike into a slowdown, but the textbook also says 3% inflation with rising expectations cannot be tolerated. The market is leaning towards a hike, and if the ECB delivers one, euro-area government bond prices (including BTPs) will come under immediate pressure. For a portfolio with 50% in fixed income, this is the single most important variable to watch. The counterargument is that the ECB may judge the inflation spike as transitory (energy-driven, not demand-driven) and hold again, which would be bond-positive but risk a credibility problem. Either way, the June meeting will likely set the direction for European fixed income through the summer. CNBC analysis
