Week in Review

A dramatic week defined by two duelling forces: collapsing oil prices as US-Iran peace talks gained traction, and fresh transatlantic trade tensions after Trump threatened "much higher" tariffs on the EU with a July 4 deadline. The net result was strongly positive for risk assets — the S&P 500 posted its sixth consecutive weekly gain to a new all-time high, while the Nikkei surged 5% to a record 62,000+. European equities were more mixed; the FTSE 100 lost ground on energy-sector weakness, while Italy's MIB outperformed on tightening spreads and solid domestic earnings.

Brent crude plunged nearly 14% from Monday's $116.55 to $100.45 by Friday as markets priced in a potential reopening of the Strait of Hormuz. Gold rallied 2% to $4,720/oz on safe-haven demand and lower real rates. In FX, the euro and sterling both recovered against the dollar, helped by a risk-on rotation and a US court ruling that questioned the legal basis of Trump's tariff authority.

Analytical Theme

The Oil-Inflation Nexus: Why This Week's Brent Move Matters More Than the Jobs Report

The 14% weekly drop in Brent crude is the single most portfolio-relevant data point this week. Since the Iran conflict began in February, elevated energy costs have been the primary driver of above-target inflation in both the Eurozone (pushing the ECB toward hikes) and the UK (keeping the BoE on hold at 3.75%). If oil stabilises around $100 — still elevated by pre-conflict standards but well below the $130 peak — it changes the calculus fundamentally.

For your fixed-income sleeve, lower oil means lower breakeven inflation, which supports bond prices and narrows BTP-Bund spreads further. For equities, it relieves margin pressure on non-energy corporates and reduces the probability of ECB hikes that could slow Eurozone growth. The key question for the week ahead: does Tuesday's CPI still reflect the old $115+ oil regime, or is the market already looking through it?
