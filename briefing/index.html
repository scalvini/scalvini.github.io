Week in Review
  • The Strait of Hormuz crisis intensified as Iran reimposed shipping restrictions after a brief ceasefire opening, sending Brent crude up 17% for the week to $105.70.
  • European equities sold off broadly, with the STOXX Europe 600 losing 2.5%, as elevated energy costs and geopolitical uncertainty weighed on sentiment.
  • The S&P 500 and Nasdaq both closed at record highs on Friday, powered by a historic Intel earnings beat that sent the chip sector soaring.
  • Consumer confidence cratered: the University of Michigan sentiment index plunged to 47.6, its lowest reading on record, while the German Ifo business climate index fell to 84.4, the weakest since May 2020.

It was a week defined by the widening gap between the US tech rally and the rest of the world. European markets buckled under the weight of the Hormuz standoff: with roughly 20% of global seaborne oil transiting the strait, its effective closure drove energy costs sharply higher and hit European industry disproportionately. Italy, where natural gas accounts for 38% of the energy mix, is especially exposed. The pan-European selloff was led by France (CAC 40 down 3.2%) and the FTSE 100 (down 2.7%), with defensive sectors like utilities and telecoms outperforming.

Across the Atlantic, the picture was starkly different. Intel's blowout Q1 results, with revenue beating estimates by over $1 billion, triggered a 24% surge in the stock and lifted the entire semiconductor sector. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq closed at fresh records on Friday, even as the Dow slipped 0.4% under pressure from energy-sensitive industrials. The divergence between US tech exceptionalism and European energy vulnerability was the dominant narrative of the week.

Both legs of the portfolio faced headwinds from the same source, the energy shock. When the dominant risk is a supply-side oil disruption, diversification between bonds and equities offers less protection than usual.

Analytical Theme: The Stagflation Trap for European Portfolios

The core challenge for any European-based investor with transatlantic exposure is that the current energy shock is simultaneously stagflationary for Europe and largely neutral for the US. The S&P 500 can rally on tech earnings even as Brent crude trades above $100, because US energy self-sufficiency insulates its economy. Europe has no such buffer. The ECB faces a genuine dilemma: hike to contain energy-driven inflation and risk tipping a weakening economy into recession, or hold and allow inflation expectations to drift upward. For a 50/50 portfolio split between euro government bonds and equity (half US, half European dividend), this creates a pernicious correlation problem. The same shock that pressures BTP spreads wider (ECB hike expectations) also weakens TDIV (European equity selloff) and erodes VOO returns (EUR strength vs USD). The traditional diversification benefit of holding both bonds and equities breaks down when the dominant risk is a supply-side energy disruption affecting all asset classes through different channels. The question for the coming weeks is whether diplomacy can provide an off-ramp, or whether the portfolio needs tactical adjustment to reduce this correlation exposure.
